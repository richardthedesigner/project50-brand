Who This Is For

Our primary audience is adults roughly 28\u201355 \u2014 often urban, professional, and previously active. They\u2019ve been fit before, or involved in sport, and are now seeking a more grounded, intelligent structure to rebuild or extend their capabilities. Secondary audiences include children and teens in structured programmes, and older adults with adjustments for safety and longevity.

Emotional Outcomes

When someone interacts with Project 50, they should feel: capable, grounded, calm, accountable, and respected as an adult. There should be an undertone of precision, structure, and control \u2014 not adrenaline, chaos, or forced positivity.

Brand Personality

If the brand were a coach, it would speak slowly, clearly, and directly. It would not shout. The personality is: measured, assured, structured, observant. Warm but not sentimental. Strong but not aggressive.

Logo A mark shaped by motion, purpose, and continuity

The Project 50 wordmark is simple in construction, but deliberate in form. The angled terminals echo intent and momentum. The customised \u201c5\u201d and \u201c0\u201d are interlinked \u2014 speaking to continuity, partnership, and enduring effort. The wordmark is used for primary brand identification; the monogram for compact applications, favicons, social avatars, and merchandise.

Usage Rules

\u2713Minimum size

Wordmark minimum width: 120px for digital, 30mm for print. Monogram minimum: 32px / 10mm.

\u2713Clear space

Maintain clear space equal to the height of the \u201c0\u201d in the wordmark on all sides. No text, images, or graphic elements may intrude.

\u2713Clean backgrounds

Use on neutral, uncluttered backgrounds. The logo benefits from generous spacing and room to breathe.

\u00d7No recolouring

Only use the approved colour variants: white on dark, black on light, black on accent. No gradients, no custom fills.

\u00d7No embellishment

Avoid drop shadows, bevels, gradients, or glowing effects. The mark stands on its own: confident and understated.

\u00d7No busy backgrounds

Never place the logo on heavily patterned or photographic backgrounds where legibility is compromised.

Typography Structured clarity, editorial weight
Primary Typeface
Jakarta

Plus Jakarta Sans is the operational typeface. It carries roughly 90% of all brand communication \u2014 from UI and microcopy to editorial and long-form writing. It\u2019s modern, structured, and legible at every size. Its personality is neutral enough not to compete, but distinctive enough not to bore.

Display Typeface
Syne

Syne is the editorial voice. Used sparingly for hero statements, campaign headlines, and editorial emphasis. Its bold geometry and off-kilter forms echo vintage sports aesthetics and progressive movements alike. It\u2019s not for paragraphs \u2014 this typeface speaks when there\u2019s something worth saying.

Pairing Rules

\u2713Chalk + Pitch

The backbone pairing. Maximum contrast, maximum clarity. Use for all body text, UI, and editorial layouts.

\u2713Pitch + Summer Tape

High-energy callout pairing. Ideal for navigation, buttons, and campaign assets. Tape on Pitch reads as confident and warm.

\u2713Chalk + Track Blue

Calm, editorial pairing. Ideal for charts, data overlays, and long-form content that needs a tonal shift from the warm core.

\u00d7Tape + Clay together

Both are warm mid-tones. Together they collapse into visual mush \u2014 no contrast, no hierarchy. Give each space to breathe.

\u00d7Steel on Steel

Steel Breath is a support colour. Don\u2019t use it for text on grey backgrounds or as a primary fill \u2014 it disappears and looks indecisive.

\u00d7Tape as background fill

Summer Tape is an accent, not a surface. Large fields of yellow feel cheap and uncontrolled. Use it for highlights, never backdrops (except the monogram card).

Imagery Strength, not spectacle

Project 50 uses two distinct imagery modes. Both share the same values: respect, substance, and an editorial eye. Never performative. Never exploitative.

B&W Performance

Strong, dynamic, close-up. We favour close crops and quiet details \u2014 a hand, a mark, a breath. Black and white doesn\u2019t mean flat: embrace contrast, grit, and grain. This is strength, not spectacle. Shoot with natural or single-source light. Let shadows do the work.

Close cropsHigh contrastReal movementGrain + texture

Monocle-Style Lifestyle

Editorial, calm, grounded. Objects and environments rather than glamour. Think soft directional light, warm neutral surfaces, resistance bands alongside notebooks. Quiet, deliberate routines with depth and tactility.

Editorial toneWarm neutralsSoft lightObjects + environments

Art Direction Notes

Composition

B&W images should feel caught, not staged. Frame for texture: skin, chalk dust, knurling, tape. Monocle images should feel arranged but effortless \u2014 like someone laid objects out with care, then stepped away.

People in Frame

Show real diversity \u2014 age, body type, skin tone \u2014 without tokenism. Avoid the camera-aware gaze. Subjects should be mid-action, focused, or at rest. Never posing, never performing for the lens.

Editorial Colour Photography

Campaign warmth

Environment + context

Voice Measured. Editorial. Anti-clich\u00e9.

The tone of voice defines how Project 50 communicates \u2014 grounded and quietly confident. It reflects the discipline of training, the clarity of structure, and the warmth of coaching. Our tone adapts to the moment, but always speaks with clarity, care, and confidence.

Writing Principles

Measured and editorial. Clear and precise. Structurally sound \u2014 good sentence rhythm and hierarchy. Confident without bombast. Human without sentimentality. Never use \u201cwe\u2019re not this, we\u2019re that\u201d structures. If it reads like an advert rather than guidance, rewrite it.

Tone by Channel

Website & App: Clear, concise, utility-first. Jakarta voice.
Email & Editorial: Warmer, longer rhythm. Still structured.
Social: Shorter, punchier. Can lean more energetic but never shout.
Campaign: Syne moments. Big, purposeful statements.
